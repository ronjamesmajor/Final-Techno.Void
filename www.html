The Evolution of the World Wide Web

The World Wide Web is a term which is used to describe the information stored on the inter-connectivity of computers across the backbone of the internet. This is a differentiation from the internet itself in that the internet is in fact the interconnected network. The web however, as it is affectionately referred to, is the series of hosted sites, information, data, and in more recent years multimedia (and secret algorithms to steal all of your personal data) which are hosted on this network.

Web 1.0: What is it?

Well back in the early days (the early nineties to the beginning of the 2000’s), people who used the internet and individuals who developed web pages did not fully realize the potential of the medium and they certainly did not call it ‘Web 1.0’. Early pages of course were text based, but it was not long before graphical web browsers such as Erwise, ViolaWWW and Mosaic were introduced. With Mosaic Web Browser, in the early days of Web 1.0, people could actually view graphical content like colors shapes and the occasional image.

Fast forward a few years, and this era was where we saw the first Browser War. Many programs were used to view the contents of the web at first, but, most notably, a browser called NetScape Navigator which saw an early rise to prominence, competed with Mosaic to become the dominant graphical web browser. Then, Microsoft released it's own version of a Web Browser called Internet Explorer. This sparked the first true competition to decide which technology was best suited to view the world wide web.

Unfortunately, like most technology in the 90’s and early 2000’s, Microsoft would not stop until it had fully realized it’s patented “Embrace, Extend, Extinguish” Mantra, and stomped out it’s competition in the web browser space.

Common web technologies during this period were things like static web pages, pages separated by frames, and exciting background images. Popular “new” web technologies during this time included the browser compatibility showdown between

Netscape's < blink> and IE's < marquee>.

Of course Marquee eventually won out. More common features which were very snazzy and cool before web developers became predatory psychopaths were things like guest-books and hit counters. This simpler time, is also when this author first learned HTML-3.0.

Web 2: Electric Boogaloo

Officially, Web 2.0 started around 2004ish, but the term was coined in 1999 by Darcy DiNucci. The idea of Web 2.0 was the idea of a connected web in which individuals could assist in the contribution of content, and connect with one another together over the world wide web. Obvious prominent examples of this technology are websites like Twitter and Facebook. However the idea was really pioneered and cultivated by Wikipedia, “web television” sites like Revision3, and social bookmarking / news sites like , and

As a personal aside, in fact,this author has very fond memories of digg.com in the early days, and even shared some drinks with the founder and host of Diggnation turned multi-millionaire angel investor Kevin Rose, as well as Diggnation co-host Alex Albrecht. Rose would later go on to found other Web 2.0 pioneering sites and technologies such as social network / micro-blog Pownce, Milk, and fasting app Zero.

Common traits of Web 2.0 sites were the ability to connect and interact with other individuals online, but it was also the beginning of “dynamic” and “responsive” web design, as well as Software as a Service, and extensive multimedia support for things like blogs, podcasts, and the framework for modern social media. In turn, YouTube was created and became a multi-billion dollar platform which houses lifetimes of “Content” for people to “consume”. This is also where the story begins to take a darker turn. The introduction of Web 2.0 meant that through constant connection, curation, and interaction, bad actors could come up with new and exciting ways to terrorize and manipulate others. Not just through person to person interaction but also via “interactive marketing”. Things started to take a turn toward data aggregation to build profiles of users likes and dislikes to serve ads better, and over time mutated into a Skinner Box of subtle behavioral manipulation via reward learning and social rewards to have the end user engage more frequently with the product (social media) and in turn, ad placement.

Web 3.0: the future is now!

Web 3.0 is still a concept which is being fleshed out and is widely discussed across many different platforms. Finding information to write about it can often be difficult, because there are dissenting opinions on what web 3.0 is really, and while some consider it the future, others say we’ve been in 3.0 for a while now. There are certain structures which do seem to carry across many definitions though, and they’re just as sinister as you might expect. Web 3.0 can be often described as being the “semantic web” in which algorithms digest and re-distribute data to one another to create a Terminator 2-esque hivemind, often regerred to as either "Skynet" or “The Singularity”. In order to have functionality so grand, a second proposed property of Web 3.0 is required.

That being of advanced Artificial Intelligence which is capable of understanding and relating data in a meaningful way. As an aside, this author recommends the written works and lectures of Jaron Lanier on this topic. Web 3.0 will require ubiquity, and will by way of adoption be ubiquitous. Yet more, it will enable to capability of sharing more than just the most modern media we have but also 3d graphics and even games could be shared across websites. Much of this technology exists today with websites that can play 3d games right in HTML5, and certainly the internet of things encapsulates the idea of ubiquity across devices. With that stated, it’s easy to see how perhaps there are many of the proposed ideas of Web 3.0 already at play today in the modern world wide web.


So what Technologies will be driving this push toward the future?

Well, in the opinion of this author:

  1. Low-Code app creation:
    For those not in the know, Low-Code or No-Code app creation is a burgeoning Web 3.0 innovation which enables users of all levels to develop or assist in the development of applications for use at home, on mobile, or in the workplace. One such example of this is Microsoft Power Apps , where utilizing the Microsoft office ecosystem, users can build sets of functionality and macros to partially automate and expedite their daily work-flow. It also ensures that users without an abundance of IT skills can get jobs done without the extensive knowledge they might have required just a few years ago.

  2. Machine Learning:
    We’ve all heard of Machine Learning by now. Advanced algorithms which can dissect and decompose massive swaths of data and then analyze and sort the data. The algorithm then internalizes this data and can more expediently access “answers” based on this data in the future. It’s the belief of this author that machine learning will be a major driver in how business and big – tech interact and work with consumers and other businesses in the future- which will drive the web more in the direction of being a tool used by companies to flip the traditional role of “user” and “content” where the content becomes the user, and the user becomes the content.

  3. Data Dignity:
    Finally, a lesser spoken about concept which may be the hope that people are so desperately looking for across the degenerate landscape of the modern world wide web. The data dignity concept is most spoken about by computer scientist, Microsoft researcher, and the father of virtual reality Jaron Lanier. The idea is that a users data has value, and so too does a users contribution to the world wide web as a user contributed medium. A dream of a world where, by decentralized controlling third parties called “Mids” (short for intermediaries), contributors and consumers alike can either be paid in small micro-payments for their data when it is used, or pay a small micro-payment to utilize data or services. Users could also choose to keep their data private and enable or disable data gathering on any and all platforms based on their preferences. Other ideas involved with data dignity include the use of software as a service models and paying postage on emails in an attempt to eliminate intentional disinformation and re-balance the ratio of truth to untruth in the online ecosystem. In very short, If it costs to spread disinformation, and there is no financial incentive for big tech to have manipulative algorithms to engage end users; things naturally get better. Moving forward into the metaverse of technology, it really is either that we force the adoption of data dignity or watch as the Matrix, Brave New World, and 1984’s Big Brother unfolds in real time over the next decade or two.
